# Principles

These principles help clubs stay useful, welcoming, and sustainable.

## Open By Default

Publish schedules, session notes, templates, and non-sensitive examples wherever possible. Use permissive licenses and make attribution easy.

Do not publish private questions, identifiable survey responses, unpublished research, sensitive data, or internal institutional information without explicit permission.

## Regular Beats Grand

A monthly session that actually happens is better than an ambitious programme that collapses after two meetings. Choose a cadence that organisers can maintain.

## Beginners Are Expected

Beginner-friendly does not mean low value. It means the club explains assumptions, avoids unnecessary jargon, and makes it safe to ask basic questions.

## Leadership Is A Practice

Facilitating a session, welcoming new members, writing notes, maintaining a calendar, and connecting two groups are all leadership work. Name these tasks and rotate them.

## Local Context Matters

Each club should fit its community. A hospital-facing bioinformatics club, an undergraduate coding circle, and an institutional research software community will need different examples, rhythms, and support.

## Shared Infrastructure Reduces Burnout

Use common templates, calendars, and documentation so every club does not have to solve the same problems alone.
